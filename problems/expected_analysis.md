## Argument 1: INVALID - Affirming the Consequent

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Affirming the Consequent

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: All mammals have hearts (All A are B)
- Premise 2: All dogs have hearts (All C are B)
- Conclusion: All dogs are mammals (All C are A)

This argument commits the fallacy of affirming the consequent. Just because all mammals have hearts, and all dogs have hearts, doesn't mean all dogs are mammals. Many other things have hearts (birds, reptiles, fish). The fact that dogs share a property with mammals doesn't make them mammals.

**Verification:** Counter-example: Fish have hearts but are not mammals.

## Argument 2: INVALID - Affirming the Consequent

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Affirming the Consequent

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: If it rains, the ground will be wet (If P then Q)
- Premise 2: The ground is wet (Q)
- Conclusion: It rained (P)

This is the classic fallacy of affirming the consequent. The ground could be wet for many reasons other than rain (someone sprayed water, a pipe burst, snow melted, etc.). The conditional only tells us what happens if it rains, not what causes the ground to be wet.

**Verification:** Consider alternative causes: sprinkler system, flooding, spilled water.

## Argument 3: INVALID - False Premise

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** False Premise (not a formal fallacy, but factually incorrect)

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: All birds can fly (FALSE - this is not universally true)
- Premise 2: Penguins are birds (TRUE)
- Conclusion: Penguins can fly (FALSE)

The logical form is valid (universal instantiation), but the first premise is factually false. Not all birds can fly - penguins, ostriches, emus, and kiwis are flightless birds.

**Verification:** Empirical observation: penguins cannot fly.

## Argument 4: INVALID - Undistributed Middle

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Undistributed Middle

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: Some philosophers are wealthy (Some A are B)
- Premise 2: Some wealthy people are politicians (Some B are C)
- Conclusion: Some philosophers are politicians (Some A are C)

This commits the fallacy of the undistributed middle. The middle term "wealthy people" is not distributed in either premise (we're only talking about "some" wealthy people in each case). The wealthy philosophers and wealthy politicians might be completely different people.

**Verification:** Venn diagram shows no necessary overlap between philosophers and politicians.

## Argument 5: INVALID - Affirming the Consequent

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Affirming the Consequent

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: If a number is even, then it is divisible by 2 (If P then Q)
- Premise 2: The number 6 is divisible by 2 (Q)
- Conclusion: 6 is even (P)

This affirms the consequent. While it's true that 6 is even, the reasoning is flawed. Many numbers are divisible by 2 that aren't even in the sense intended (e.g., we could define "even" more restrictively). More importantly, the logical form is invalid regardless of the conclusion's truth.

**Verification:** The argument form is invalid, even if the conclusion happens to be true.

## Argument 6: VALID

**Classification:** VALID

**Fallacy:** None

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: All squares are rectangles (All A are B)
- Premise 2: All rectangles have four sides (All B are C)
- Conclusion: All squares have four sides (All A are C)

This is a valid syllogism in the form of Barbara (AAA-1). If all A are B, and all B are C, then all A are C. The middle term "rectangles" is properly distributed.

**Verification:** This follows the transitive property of inclusion.

## Argument 7: INVALID - Hasty Generalization / Causal Fallacy

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Hasty Generalization / Correlation vs Causation

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: The more you study, the better you do on exams (general correlation)
- Premise 2: John studies more than anyone else
- Conclusion: John will do the best on the exam

This assumes a perfect correlation and ignores other factors. The first premise states a general trend, not a universal rule. Other factors affect exam performance: natural ability, test anxiety, sleep, health, understanding of material, etc.

**Verification:** Consider confounding variables: prior knowledge, test-taking skills, mental state.

## Argument 8: VALID

**Classification:** VALID

**Fallacy:** None

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: All cats are mammals (All A are B)
- Premise 2: No reptiles are mammals (No C are B)
- Conclusion: No cats are reptiles (No A are C)

This is a valid syllogism (Celarent form). If all cats are mammals, and no reptiles are mammals, then cats and reptiles are disjoint sets. No cat can be a reptile.

**Verification:** This follows from the law of non-contradiction.

## Argument 9: INVALID - Affirming the Consequent

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** Affirming the Consequent

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: If you work hard, you will succeed (If P then Q)
- Premise 2: Alice succeeded (Q)
- Conclusion: Alice worked hard (P)

Classic affirming the consequent. Alice could have succeeded through luck, connections, inherited wealth, or other means. The conditional only tells us that hard work leads to success, not that success implies hard work.

**Verification:** Consider alternative paths to success: luck, privilege, inheritance.

## Argument 10: INVALID - False Premise / Hasty Generalization

**Classification:** INVALID

**Fallacy:** False Premise (and potentially Hasty Generalization)

**Analysis:**
- Premise 1: All politicians are liars (All A are B)
- Premise 2: My brother is a politician (C is A)
- Conclusion: My brother is a liar (C is B)

The logical form is valid (universal instantiation), but the first premise is both:
1. Factually false (not all politicians are liars)
2. A hasty generalization (overgeneralizing from some politicians to all politicians)

**Verification:** Counter-examples exist of honest politicians.
